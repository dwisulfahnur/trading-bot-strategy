The Williams fractal indicator is a
powerful tool for identifying key levels
in the market. In this guide, we'll
explore how to use it to build
profitable day trading strategies.
Williams fractal is a simple but very
useful indicator that helps identify key
support and resistance levels. If a top
fractal appears, it marks a potential
resistance level. If a bottom fractal
appears, it marks a potential support
level. How is a fractal formed? A top
fractal appears when the high of a
candle is higher than the highs of the
two candles to the left and two to the
right. A bottom fractal appears when the
low of the candle is lower than the lows
of the two candles to the left and two
to the right. An important note, a
fractal forms with a delay of two
candles because the indicator only
confirms after those candles close. The
problem with the standard indicator is
that it shows too many fractals, which
can be confusing. On an active market
such as the 1-hour chart, you might see
dozens of fractals, many of which don't
provide reliable signals. The solution
is to use a custom indicator such as
Williams trailing stop-loss. It allows
you to change the fractal range. I
recommend disabling unnecessary lines in
the settings and increasing the range
from 5 to 9 candles. This helps filter
out noise, leaving only the key levels,
making your analysis cleaner and your
trading decisions easier. What not to
do? Beginners often use fractals as
reversal signals. A bottom fractal
appears, they buy. A top fractal
appears, they sell. In practice, this
strategy gives a low percentage of
profitable trades and does not perform
well. Instead, I recommend using
fractalss as breakout signals, filtering
them with a trend using the 200 EMA. How
does this work? Add the 200 EMA to
define the trend. If price is above the
200 EMA, focus on top fractals for
buying opportunities. If price is below
the 200 EMA, focus on bottom fractals
for selling opportunities. Wait for a
candle to close above the last top
fractal for a buy or below the last
bottom fractal for a sell. Set your stop
loss below the entry candle for buys or
above it for sells. Take profit should
be 1 and a half times the size of your
stop-loss. Example one, price is above
the 200 EMA. A breakout of the top
fractal occurs. This gives us a buy
signal. Place the stop loss below the
entry candle and set the take-profit one
and a half times the stop. Example two.
Price is below the 200 EMA. A breakout
of the bottom fractal occurs. This gives
us a sell signal. Place the stop loss
above the entry candle and set the
takerit 1 and 1/2 times the stop. Now
you know how to use Williams fractal as
a breakout strategy. For more accurate
entries, combine it with the alligator
and stochastic RSI indicators. Start by
identifying the trend using the
alligator. All three lines should be
moving in the same direction and be
separated. If all three lines are
pointing up, the market is in an uptrend
and we look for by trades. If they are
pointing down, it's a downtrend and we
look for sells. Avoid sideways markets
where the lines are crossing each other.
Entry condition. In an uptrend, wait for
a bottom fractal to appear while the
stoastic RSI is in the oversold zone
below 20. In a downtrend, wait for a top
fractal while the stoastic RSI is in the
overbought zone above 80. Stop loss
should be set below the fractal for buys
and above it for sells. Close the trade
when price reaches the alligator's
middle line. Example, the market is in
an uptrend, a bottom fractal forms, and
the stochastic RSI is oversold. Enter a
buy trade. When price reaches the
alligator's middle line, close the
position and take profit. You now have
two effective fractalbased strategies.
The first is a breakout strategy
filtered by the 200 EMA. The second is a
trend following pullback strategy
confirmed by the alligator and
stochastic RSI. Both strategies are easy
to apply and can be adapted to your
trading system. The key is to be
patient, filter your signals carefully,
and follow riskmanagement rules.
